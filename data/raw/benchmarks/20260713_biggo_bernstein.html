China's Semiconductor Equipment Localization Rate Jumps to 21%: Etching Tools Enter TSMC Lines, Lithography Remains the Final Hurdle

Add to Google Preferred Sources
China's semiconductor equipment localization rate has surged from 4% in 2018 to 21% in 2025, forming a three-tier battlefield. Etching equipment leads with a 31% localization rate, with Advanced Micro-Fabrication Equipment Inc. China (AMEC) tools already deployed in TSMC's 5nm production lines. Thin-film deposition and cleaning equipment are accelerating rapidly, with NAURA Technology Group posting approximately $2.2 billion in first-half 2025 revenue, ranking seventh globally among equipment suppliers. Lithography and related segments remain below 10% localization, though Shanghai Micro Electronics Equipment (SMEE) has delivered its first 28nm immersion lithography machine. Meanwhile, China's electronic specialty gases sector—dubbed the "lifeblood" of semiconductor manufacturing—is entering a golden era of domestic substitution, with Huate Gas and CSSC Specialty Gases breaking overseas monopolies in high-purity gases and photolithography gases, deeply embedding themselves in the supply chains of leading global wafer fabs.
Key Elements

4%, 13%, 21%. These three figures trace the decade-long trajectory of China's semiconductor equipment industry breakthrough. According to Bernstein and multiple public institutions, China's semiconductor equipment localization rate has climbed from approximately 4% in 2018 to roughly 13% in 2024, reaching about 21% in 2025. A decade ago, nearly every core piece of equipment in Chinese wafer fabs relied on imports; a decade later, AMEC's etching machines are deployed in volume at TSMC's 5nm and below advanced production lines. From near-zero to penetrating the world's most advanced manufacturing floors, this forced march through sanctions has moved from "can we build it?" into deeper waters defined by "which fortresses have fallen, and which chasms remain to be crossed?"

Deconstructing this domestic substitution campaign, semiconductor equipment can be divided into three tiers of battle. The first tier is etching equipment—a textbook case of domestic substitution—with a current localization rate of approximately 31%. The global etching equipment market was long dominated by Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, and Applied Materials, which together controlled over 90% of the market. Today, AMEC's plasma etching tools cover 5nm and below process nodes and have successfully entered TSMC's production lines. NAURA Technology Group (002371.SZ) etching equipment has also entered SMIC's (688981.SH) advanced production lines. A telling footnote: as early as 2015, the U.S. Department of Commerce lifted export controls on plasma etching equipment to China, indirectly confirming that domestically produced etching tools had reached a level capable of competing head-to-head with international giants. The fact that etching—the "toughest bone to chew"—has been conquered proves that with national resolve and industrial coordination, the blockade on core equipment is not unbreakable.

The second tier is thin-film deposition and cleaning equipment, currently in an accelerated ramp-up phase. Thin-film deposition equipment localization stands at roughly 27%, while cleaning equipment has exceeded 50% in certain sub-segments. NAURA has established a full portfolio spanning physical vapor deposition (PVD), chemical vapor deposition (CVD), and atomic layer deposition (ALD), with its thin-film deposition equipment revenue surpassing 10 billion yuan (approximately $1.5 billion) in 2024. Piotech's plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) equipment also holds a leading market position. These segments are rapidly advancing from meeting mature node requirements to challenging advanced process nodes. Meanwhile, the platformization trend is strengthening the comprehensive competitiveness of domestic equipment. According to CICC data, NAURA posted first-half 2025 revenue of approximately $2.2 billion, up 31% year-on-year, ranking seventh among global equipment suppliers. Chinese equipment manufacturers are evolving from single-category suppliers into platform-type enterprises capable of delivering multi-node solutions, with customer stickiness steadily increasing.

The third tier—and the final chasm standing before full-scale breakthrough—encompasses lithography, coating and developing, and metrology/inspection equipment, where localization rates generally remain below 10%. Lithography machines are called the "pinnacle of industrial manufacturing," with the difficulty lying not in any single technological breakthrough but in the systemic integration of over a hundred disciplines including optics, precision mechanics, materials, and control systems, heavily dependent on coordination across the world's top-tier supply chains. But the ice is beginning to crack. In 2025, Shanghai Micro Electronics Equipment (SMEE) delivered its first 28nm immersion lithography machine. According to public reports, the equipment achieved over 70% domestic content and can support 7nm multi-patterning processes. This marks a critical leap for domestically produced lithography from "prototype" to "usable," though a considerable distance remains from the most advanced extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines dominated by ASML. The chasm persists, but the bridge has begun construction.

While equipment advances, electronic specialty gases—the "flowing blood" of semiconductor manufacturing—are also entering a golden cycle of domestic substitution. Electronic specialty gases run through the entire process of deposition, etching, and doping, with purity requirements routinely reaching 99.9999999%, where trace impurities can determine the yield of an entire wafer. As the AI computing wave sweeps through global wafer fabs, etching steps at 7nm and below advanced nodes are seven times those of 65nm mature processes. HBM high-bandwidth memory and 200-plus-layer 3D NAND stacked chips have dramatically increased per-die gas consumption, with the gas usage scale for a single high-end memory chip more than double that of traditional products. China's domestic electronic gas market has climbed from 10.5 billion yuan in 2017 to 19.5 billion yuan (approximately $2.9 billion) in 2024, with institutions projecting the overall scale will exceed 25 billion yuan (approximately $3.7 billion) by 2028.

Supply-demand mismatches have opened a massive window of opportunity for domestically produced gases. Overseas supply chains face multiple constraints: Middle East conflicts have damaged helium equipment in Qatar, and Russia has imposed helium export controls extending through end-2027. China's export controls on high-purity tungsten have directly caused inventory emergencies for Japanese and South Korean tungsten hexafluoride manufacturers, with core product prices surging 236% year-on-year. Capacity contractions among overseas giants and geopolitical controls on raw materials are creating cracks in a landscape long dominated by foreign oligopolies. Chinese enterprises are establishing differentiated positions along the industry chain. Upstream, Guanggang Gases (688548.SH), as the only domestic Chinese enterprise holding multi-region long-term helium agreements, has deployed nearly 100 liquid helium cold boxes and simultaneously built supporting on-site gas stations at over a dozen 12-inch wafer fabs. Sanfu Shares (603938.SH) is deeply engaged in silicon-based precursors such as dichlorosilane and trichlorosilane, with electronic-grade silane precursors already supplied in volume to domestic memory and logic chip plants.

The midstream represents the core battleground for industry value. CSSC Specialty Gases (688146.SH) holds world-class tungsten hexafluoride production capacity, currently at 2,000 tons with plans to reach a total capacity of 3,000 tons by 2027. Its 6N-grade high-purity products are stably supplied to major memory manufacturers both domestically and overseas, and select photolithography gases from its portfolio have passed certification by an ASML subsidiary. Huate Gas (688268.SH) stands as the benchmark enterprise in China's photolithography gas sector—the only domestic manufacturer with multiple rare-gas-mix photolithography gases simultaneously certified by both ASML and Japan's GIGAPHOTON. Its products now cover the full range of 3D NAND and HBM high-end memory customers and have penetrated the global supply chains of Intel, TSMC, and SMIC.

For semiconductor equipment domestic substitution, the core proposition over the past five years has been "from 0 to 1, solving existence." Over the next three to five years, the proposition will shift to "from 1 to N, solving quality." The competition will no longer be about prototype specifications in the lab, but about stability, yield, and cost control in mass production environments, along with service capabilities built on vast process experience. This means the breakthrough will not be a blitzkrieg but an industrial war of attrition testing endurance. Lithography—the final and hardest chasm—will most likely need to be filled inch by inch through time, orders, and generations of engineers. Judging the true progress of domestic substitution cannot rely solely on the noise of isolated "breakthroughs"; one must look at whether equipment has entered volume production lines and secured repeat orders from wafer fabs.
